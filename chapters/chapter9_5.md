**Chapter 9.5: The Scholar and the Rakshasa**

A few weeks had passed since the unsettling incident in Ranchi, and life
had gradually settled back into a comfortable rhythm. Arjun's factory
was finally back under his firm control, operating smoother than ever.
The local authorities, who had previously shown frustratingly little
interest in helping him, suddenly became remarkably cooperative and
efficient. As for the sudden disappearance of the goons, nobody openly
discussed it, and frankly, nobody wanted to pry into matters best left
alone. Instead, a much happier matter occupied everyone\'s attention:
the upcoming wedding.

Since both families had enthusiastically agreed to the marriage,
preparations were progressing at a steady and joyous pace. Phone calls
between the households became a daily ritual, and visits grew
increasingly frequent. Long discussions about venues, expansive guest
lists, vibrant decorations, and traditional ceremonies pleasantly filled
many of their evenings.

One lively Saturday, Advik made his way to Chetana's family home. The
moment he stepped through the door, Siri welcomed him with the
overflowing warmth typical of her deeply affectionate nature.

\"Come, come!\" she ushered him inside, immediately inspecting his
appearance. \"You look thinner than the last time you were here. Are you
even eating properly?\".

Advik offered a helpless, endearing smile. \"Yes, Aunty, I promise I
am.\".

\"I simply don\'t believe you,\" she retorted playfully, already
planning his dinner.

Before Advik could mount a proper defense, Chaitanya appeared from down
the hall, a mischievous glint in his eye. \"Akka, your Rakshasa has
arrived!\" he announced loudly.

Chetana immediately stepped forward and smacked her younger brother on
the back of the head. \"Idiot,\" she scolded.

Chaitanya just grinned, rubbing his head, clearly enjoying the chaos he
stirred. \"What? It\'s true! He only works at night and barely sleeps
during the day,\" he teased.

Advik coughed awkwardly, trying to hide his sudden discomfort, while
Chetana simply laughed at her brother\'s antics.

For the next few hours, the house remained an absolute whirlwind of
lively energy. Siri fussed over Advik constantly, making sure his plate
was never empty, while Chaitanya kept finding new ways to playfully
tease him. Chetana thoroughly enjoyed watching her future husband
struggle gracefully through the overwhelming wave of familial attention.

However, amidst the joyful noise, one person remained unusually quiet:
Jagannath. The esteemed scholar simply observed. He observed carefully,
utilizing the keen analytical outlook he had honed over years of
studying scriptures, which had taught him to notice the intricate
details that others so easily overlooked. He watched for the small
things, the tiny inconsistencies that most would quickly brush off. They
were the sort of details that, when pieced together, slowly formed
undeniable patterns.

Throughout the day, the patriarch noticed several unusual habits. For
one, Advik appeared remarkably more energetic as the sun began to set.
Furthermore, the young man\'s hearing seemed extraordinarily sharp.
Twice during their animated conversations, Advik physically reacted to
faint sounds long before anyone else in the room even registered them.
He noticed a motorcycle rolling to a stop on the street outside. He
heard a pressure cooker's high-pitched whistle from a neighboring house.
He even caught the sound of a stray dog barking several streets away.
Every single time, Advik's attention shifted immediately and
instinctively, while nobody else in the family seemed to notice a thing.
Jagannath did.

Later that evening, as they all sat down for dinner, Siri casually
mentioned an upcoming visit she was planning to a local Vishnu temple.
For a brief, fleeting moment, Advik's expression visibly tightened into
one of profound discomfort. It was there only for a fraction of a second
before it completely vanished, hidden behind a polite smile. Most
ordinary people would have completely missed the subtle shift. Jagannath
didn\'t.

The perceptive scholar also noticed another distinct pattern. Whenever
their dinnertime discussions drifted toward the Puranas and ancient
texts, Advik actively and knowledgeably participated. Except, of course,
when the topic specifically touched upon Rakshasas. Then, the young man
became strangely, heavily silent. He didn\'t appear uncomfortable or
confused by the subject; rather, he seemed incredibly careful. It was as
though he was meticulously measuring the weight of every single word
before choosing not to speak at all.

By the time the dinner plates were cleared, Jagannath had begun
connecting the disparate pieces together. Individually, each isolated
observation meant absolutely nothing. But together? They painted a
remarkably unusual, almost impossible picture. Here sat a distinctly
nocturnal young man who possessed extraordinary senses. He exhibited an
undeniable discomfort around mentions of Bhagwan Vishnu, and maintained
a strange, calculated silence regarding Rakshasas.

And then, there was Chetana herself. He noted her protective reaction
whenever the Puranas surfaced in conversation. He caught the subtle,
knowing glances she quickly exchanged with Advik. He observed the
incredibly careful way both of them actively maneuvered to avoid certain
subjects.

Despite putting all this together, Jagannath said nothing during the
meal. But the astonishing conclusion rapidly forming in his intellectual
mind became increasingly difficult for him to ignore.

As the night deepened, the lively house gradually fell silent. Lights
were switched off one by one, doors gently closed, and the echoing
conversations finally faded into peaceful slumber. Around midnight,
Jagannath woke briefly, needing a drink of water. As he walked quietly
past the glass doors of the balcony, he noticed a solitary figure
sitting outside in the cool air.

It was Advik. The young man sat completely alone under the vast expanse
of the night sky. He was completely awake and entirely relaxed, acting
as though the stroke of midnight were merely the middle of a lazy
afternoon. Jagannath quietly pushed open the door and stepped outside.

Advik\'s heightened senses caught the movement immediately. \"Uncle,\"
he greeted softly.

\"Couldn\'t sleep?\" Jagannath asked, pulling up a chair nearby.

Advik offered a small, knowing smile. \"You could say that.\".

Jagannath chuckled softly, settling into his seat. \"I suspected as
much.\".

For several long moments, the two men sat in a comfortable silence. The
cool night breeze moved gently through the rustling leaves of the trees.
Far away in the sleeping city, a dog barked. Somewhere in the distance,
the mournful horn of a passing train echoed through the dark. Instantly,
Advik's attention instinctively shifted toward the faint sound, his
posture adjusting just a fraction.

Jagannath noticed it again. In that silent moment, the scholar's final,
lingering doubts completely disappeared. At last, he decided it was time
to speak.

\"You know,\" Jagannath began, keeping his tone light and casual, \"I
have spent most of my life deeply studying the scriptures.\".

Advik nodded respectfully. \"Chetana told me.\".

\"The Puranas contain records of many fascinating beings,\" the older
man continued, watching the night sky.

\"They do,\" Advik agreed cautiously.

\"Devatas.\". \"Yes.\". \"Asuras.\". \"Yes.\". \"Yakshas.\". \"Yes.\".
\"Rakshasas.\".

The heavy word lingered between them, hanging in the cool midnight air.
For the very first time that night, Advik became completely, unnaturally
still. Jagannath looked over at the young man with a calm, steady gaze.
There was no accusing glare, nor was there any fearful trembling. He was
simply calm.

\"I think,\" Jagannath said gently, breaking the tension, \"that I have
finally solved a very complex puzzle.\".

The silence that followed stretched on for several excruciating seconds.
Then, Advik let out a long, slow exhale. He understood the situation
immediately: the scholar knew. He might not know absolutely everything,
but he knew enough. He knew far more than most ordinary humans ever
would or ever should. For a tense moment, neither of them spoke as Advik
deliberately looked away, making the tranquil night suddenly feel
overwhelmingly heavy.

\"I suppose I shouldn\'t be surprised,\" Advik finally said, his voice
quiet and resigned.

Jagannath offered a gentle smile. \"No.\".

\"You figured it out.\".

\"Eventually,\" the scholar replied lightly.

Another profound silence followed as Advik stared deeply into the
enveloping darkness. He had imagined this exact moment playing out in
his head many times over the years. He hadn\'t specifically pictured it
happening with Jagannath, but he had prepared for the inevitable moment
people in general learned his truth. He had braced himself for the fear,
the disgust, the harsh rejection, and the deep suspicion. He had built
walls to protect himself from all of those expected reactions.

What he had absolutely not prepared himself for, however, was this calm,
unwavering acceptance. Jagannath remained sitting in his chair,
perfectly relaxed and at ease.

\"So,\" Advik finally asked, turning to face him, \"you\'re not
afraid?\".

Jagannath laughed softly, a warm and genuine sound. \"My boy, after
spending decades deeply immersed in reading about Rakshasas, finding one
casually sitting on my balcony is far less shocking than you might
imagine.\".

Despite the gravity of the situation, Advik found himself laughing as
well. The suffocating tension in the air eased slightly. Jagannath
leaned back in his chair, his expression turning a bit more serious.

\"Tell me something honestly,\" the scholar requested.

Advik nodded solemnly. \"Anything.\".

\"Have you ever harmed anyone without just reason?\". \"No,\" Advik
answered firmly. \"Do you enjoy hurting people?\". \"No.\". \"Do you
truly care about Chetana?\".

The answer came instantly, filled with an undeniable fierce conviction.
\"More than anything in this world.\".

Jagannath smiled warmly. \"Then most of my fatherly concerns are already
perfectly resolved.\".

Advik looked at the older man in sheer surprise, struggling to
comprehend his lack of judgment.

Jagannath continued, his voice adopting the soothing cadence of a
teacher. \"The ancient scriptures teach us many profound things. Among
the most important of these lessons is the fact that one\'s character
matters far more than their birth.\". He paused thoughtfully.
\"Vibhishana, after all, was a Rakshasa.\".

Advik nodded slowly, acknowledging the historical reference.

\"And frankly, many humans have behaved far worse than any monsters
described in the texts,\" Jagannath added.

Again, Advik nodded in silent agreement.

Jagannath looked directly into Advik\'s eyes, his voice remaining calm
and reassuringly steady. \"I judge people purely by what they choose to
do. I have seen that you protect others. I see that you genuinely love
my daughter. You clearly possess great power, yet you choose to practice
restraint. You could so easily misuse your extraordinary abilities to
take whatever you want, but you consciously choose not to.\".

Advik listened quietly, overwhelmed by the depth of the scholar\'s
words. Every single sentence struck much deeper than he ever expected,
largely because this incredible acceptance wasn\'t stemming from blind
ignorance. It was coming from a place of profound understanding.
Jagannath knew exactly what Advik was down to his core, and he accepted
him anyway.

For several emotional moments, Advik couldn\'t find his voice to speak.
Finally, he lowered his head in a gesture of deep respect. \"Thank
you,\" he murmured, the words coming out much softer and more vulnerable
than he had intended.

Jagannath smiled affectionately. \"You don\'t ever need my permission to
simply be a good man.\".

\"Still,\" Advik admitted, his voice full of gratitude, \"it means a lot
to hear it.\".

The older man nodded understandingly. \"I know it does.\".

Advik turned his gaze toward the quiet, sleeping house, specifically
looking toward the room where Chetana rested peacefully. He then turned
back to meet Jagannath\'s eyes with intense seriousness. \"I promise you
something.\".

The scholar waited patiently.

\"No matter what happens in the future, I will protect her,\" Advik
vowed, the statement carrying an unusually heavy, almost ancient weight.
It wasn\'t merely a romantic promise made by a smitten fiancé. It
certainly wasn\'t a standard ceremonial promise made for tradition\'s
sake. It was a Rakshasa\'s promise---bound by blood and absolute honor.

Jagannath understood the gravity of the oath perfectly.

\"And our family,\" Advik added softly, ensuring there was no ambiguity.
\"I will protect them too.\".

The older man smiled, his eyes crinkling with genuine warmth. \"I
believe you completely.\".

For a while longer, neither of them felt the need to speak. The shared
silence between them felt comfortable and deeply peaceful. Then,
breaking the quiet, Jagannath unexpectedly shifted the conversation.

\"There is, however, one thing that still concerns me,\" he said
thoughtfully.

Advik immediately knew the topic before it was even spoken. \"Vishnu.\".

Jagannath nodded slowly.

The younger man\'s expression tightened instantly, his defensive walls
springing back up. That visceral reaction alone told the seasoned
scholar absolutely everything he needed to know.

\"You are terrified of Him,\" Jagannath stated matter-of-factly.

Advik looked away, staring off into the middle distance. \"Maybe.\".

\"That wasn\'t a denial,\" the scholar pointed out.

\"No,\" Advik conceded quietly.

Jagannath studied the young man carefully before speaking with extreme
gentleness. \"For someone who actively fears Vishnu so much, you are
remarkably unlike the ancient Rakshasas who tragically earned His divine
wrath.\".

Advik said nothing in response, simply absorbing the observation.

\"You fiercely protect people,\" Jagannath listed. \"You actively avoid
unnecessary violence whenever possible. You show remarkable restraint in
everything you do.\". Jagannath slowly shook his head. \"I really don\'t
think Vishnu is your enemy.\".

Advik remained unconvinced by the comforting words. The deep-seated fear
ran far too deep in his veins; it was far too instinctive and incredibly
old to be washed away by a simple conversation. Yet, despite the
paralyzing fear, he listened intently because he deeply respected the
wise man speaking to him.

*One day,* Jagannath thought to himself, *he will finally understand.*.
The scholar smiled faintly at the thought. \"Perhaps someday you\'ll be
able to see that truth for yourself,\" he offered kindly.

Advik looked up toward the vast canopy of stars. Perhaps. But definitely
not today. Today, the ancient fear firmly remained. Still, for the very
first time in his exceptionally long life, someone had managed to plant
a tiny seed of doubt within that towering fear. And perhaps, that small
seed was exactly what mattered most right now.

Eventually, Jagannath stood up, smoothing his clothes. \"It\'s late, my
boy.\".

Advik rose to his feet as well. The older man reached out and placed a
reassuring, fatherly hand on his broad shoulder. \"You have my full
blessing.\".

For a brief, stunning moment, Advik simply stared at him, overwhelmed by
the magnitude of the gift. Then, he bowed deeply and respectfully.
\"Thank you, Uncle,\" he said, his voice thick with emotion.

Jagannath smiled warmly, his eyes softening as he looked at the man who
would marry his daughter. \"No,\" he corrected gently. \"Welcome to the
family.\".

For the first time that entire night, under the watchful gaze of the
moon, Advik felt completely and utterly at peace. As the scholar quietly
returned inside the house and the Rakshasa remained alone beneath the
silent stars, neither of them truly realized just how monumentally
important this midnight conversation would eventually become.

Months later, far away in the sacred city of Srirangam, its final,
profound meaning would finally be completely understood.
