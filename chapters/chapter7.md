**Chapter 7: The Fear**

Exactly three days had peacefully passed since the unforgettable,
terrifying incident on the secluded hilltop. It had been three long days
since Chetana had watched her ordinary boyfriend violently transform
into something that logic dictated should not have existed. Three days
had passed since she had finally learned the absolute truth: Advik was a
Rakshasa.

Yet, strangely enough, the primary thing occupying her racing thoughts
was not the horrifying physical transformation itself; rather, it was
how remarkably normal Advik had remained in the aftermath. He still
cracked terrible, groan-inducing jokes, he still sent her random
messages at incredibly odd hours of the quiet night, and he still
ruthlessly teased her whenever he got the slightest opportunity. The man
had somehow casually revealed that he could physically transform into a
giant, nightmarish man-lion, only to immediately go right back to
passionately arguing about whether pineapple belonged on pizza. Chetana
found the entire situation absolutely ridiculous, yet simultaneously,
she found it oddly comforting.

A fond smile played on her lips as she walked briskly through the
brightly lit office corridor. Suddenly, a message notification chimed on
her phone.

\"Night shift starts in ten minutes. Pray for me,\" Advik wrote.

Chetana immediately typed out a reply. \"You literally prefer the night
shift.\".

\"That\'s not the point,\" he countered seconds later.

\"What\'s the point then?\" she asked.

A reply arrived instantly: \"I wanted sympathy.\".

Chetana shook her head affectionately, muttering \"Idiot\" under her
breath before putting her phone away and returning to her work.

Later that pleasant evening, they met at their usual small tea stall
located just a short walk from the office. The roadside stall was
incredibly crowded as usual, with tired people standing around chatting
loudly while drinking hot tea from steaming paper cups. Advik leaned
casually against his parked bike, looking entirely at ease in the
bustling environment.

Chetana gratefully accepted the warm cup he handed her. \"Thank you.\".

\"You\'re welcome,\" he replied softly.

They stood quietly together for a long moment, allowing the cool evening
breeze to drift comfortably through the busy street. Then, Chetana
finally decided to ask the burning question that had been severely
bothering her for days.

\"So,\" she began carefully.

Advik immediately narrowed his eyes in suspicion. \"That tone means
trouble.\".

She deliberately ignored his observation. \"You told me you\'re a
Rakshasa.\".

\"Correct,\" he nodded.

\"You can shapeshift,\" she continued.

\"Also correct,\" he agreed easily.

\"You turned into a giant man-lion,\" she stated.

\"Still correct,\" he confirmed.

Chetana took a slow, thoughtful sip of her tea. \"Is there anything else
I should know?\".

Advik immediately looked away, breaking their steady eye contact. That
simple evasion alone instantly caught her attention, as normally he
answered absolutely everything with unwavering confidence. This time,
however, he seemed visibly uncomfortable---very uncomfortable.

\"Chetana\...\" he started hesitantly.

\"Hmm?\".

\"Can we please discuss literally anything else?\" he pleaded.

Her eyes narrowed with determination. \"No.\".

Advik let out a heavy sigh. She had learned very quickly that this
particular, defeated expression usually meant he was about to completely
lose an argument.

\"What is it?\" she pressed gently but firmly.

He remained stubbornly silent for several tense seconds before finally
muttering something completely inaudible.

\"What?\" she asked.

\"I said it\'s stupid,\" he mumbled.

\"What is?\".

\"The thing,\" he vaguely offered.

\"What thing?\" she demanded.

Advik stared intensely at the surface of his tea. \"The thing I really
don\'t want to talk about,\" he clarified unhelpfully.

Chetana firmly folded her arms across her chest. \"I am not letting this
go,\" she warned.

\"I know,\" he sighed.

\"Then tell me,"She instructed.

Advik cautiously looked around the crowded street to ensure nobody was
actively listening to their conversation. Then, he deliberately lowered
his voice to a quiet murmur. \"I don\'t like Vishnu temples.\".

Chetana blinked in mild surprise, as that was certainly not the dark,
dangerous confession she had expected to hear. \"That\'s it?\" she
asked.

Advik immediately looked highly offended. \"\'That\'s it\'?\" he
repeated indignantly.

\"Yes,\" she said.

\"You think that\'s normal?\" he challenged.

\"You just don\'t like temples?\" she reasoned.

\"No, that\'s the weird part. I\'m actually perfectly fine with
temples,\" he corrected.

Chetana frowned in confusion. \"What?\".

\"I like Shiva temples,\" he revealed.

Now she was even more confused by the bizarre distinction. \"You like
Shiva temples?\".

\"Yeah,\" Advik casually shrugged. \"I don\'t mind them at all.
Honestly, I strongly prefer them.\".

\"Prefer them?\" she echoed.

\"They feel\... peaceful,\" he explained softly.

Chetana stared at him, trying to process the illogical contradiction.
\"So, you are completely okay with Shiva temples but inexplicably scared
of Vishnu temples?\".

\"Exactly,\" he confirmed.

\"That makes absolutely no sense,\" she stated plainly.

\"I know,\" he agreed.

\"You are impossible,\" she sighed.

\"I\'ve been told that before,\" he replied. Despite the lighthearted
joke, his expression quickly shifted, becoming intensely serious once
again.

\"But why?\" she pushed gently.

Advik hesitated, struggling to find the right words. Then, he very
quietly confessed, \"I\'m scared of them.\".

Chetana almost dropped her paper cup of tea in pure shock. \"What?\".

\"I\'m scared of Vishnu,\" he admitted.

A heavy, profound silence fell between them. The surrounding noise of
the bustling traffic seemed to completely fade away into the background.
For the very first time since she had met him, Chetana saw genuine,
undeniable fear shining brightly in Advik\'s eyes. It was not mere
discomfort, nor was it embarrassment; it was fear---raw, primal, and
deeply instinctive. It was the kind of terror that absolutely could not
be faked.

\"You mean\...\" she began, still struggling to comprehend.

Advik nodded slowly. \"Bhagwan Vishnu,\" he clarified.

Chetana simply stared at him in awe. This was the exact same man who had
stood perfectly calmly in front of a violent group of aggressive men.
This was the same incredible man who could transform into a terrifying
creature straight out of the ancient Puranas. This was the same
immensely powerful man who could probably tear apart a heavy vehicle
with his bare hands. And despite all of that, he was deeply afraid of
Vishnu?.

\"Why?\" she asked softly, her tone full of genuine empathy.

\"I honestly don\'t know,\" he answered truthfully.

\"You don\'t know?\".

\"I\'ve never known,\" he replied, his voice becoming remarkably
quieter. \"It has always been there, ever since my childhood.\".

Chetana listened carefully, giving him the space to explain his
vulnerability.

Advik continued, \"Whenever I go anywhere near a Vishnu temple, I
instantly feel uneasy.\".

\"Uneasy in what way?\" she pressed.

\"Like something terribly bad is about to happen to me,\" he elaborated.

\"But what about Shiva temples?\" she asked, circling back to the odd
exception.

\"They don\'t bother me in the slightest,\" he confirmed.

\"Not at all?\".

\"Not really,\" he mused, pausing for a moment. \"In fact, I generally
feel more comfortable standing there than practically anywhere else.\".

That revelation only served to make the already bizarre situation feel
even stranger. \"But why?\" she asked again.

\"I don\'t know,\" he repeated, his strong fingers tightening nervously
around the flimsy paper cup. \"I just know it.\".

The answer sounded entirely irrational to her logical mind, yet the
profound fear reflecting in his golden eyes was undeniably real. It was
painfully real.

Chetana suddenly remembered something deeply personal from her own
upbringing. She vividly recalled her devoted mother, the countless daily
prayers, and the numerous Vishnu temples they had peacefully visited
together throughout her entire childhood. Nothing terrible had ever
happened in those sacred spaces---not even once.

She reached out and gently touched his arm in a comforting gesture.
\"Advik.\".

\"Hmm?\" he responded, still tense.

\"Maybe you are just worrying for absolutely no reason,\" she suggested.

His defensive reaction was immediate. \"No.\".

The answer came out far too quickly and sounded entirely too sharp.
Chetana blinked in surprise as Advik deliberately looked away from her.

\"No,\" he repeated firmly. \"You really don\'t understand.\".

\"Then please explain it to me,\" she requested.

\"I can\'t,\" he sighed.

\"Just try,\" she urged.

He remained agonizingly silent for a long moment before finally
whispering, \"What if He notices me?\".

Chetana simply stared at him, taken aback. That haunting statement
sounded far less like a simple question and much more like a waking
nightmare that had relentlessly followed him throughout his entire life.

\"What do you honestly think will happen if He does?\" she asked softly.

\"I don\'t know,\" he admitted.

\"Then why automatically assume the absolute worst?\" she challenged.

\"Because keeping me safe from the worst is exactly what my instincts
are for,\" he fired back.

The protective words emerged immediately, almost entirely reflexively.
Chetana acutely noticed that his breathing had become slightly uneven,
his broad shoulders were incredibly tense, and his eyes kept darting
around. It was vividly apparent as though merely discussing the topic
itself made him feel physically unsafe.

She affectionately squeezed his hand to ground him. \"I promise you,
nothing bad is going to happen,\" she assured him.

Advik let out a nervous, utterly humorless laugh. \"You sound very
confident about that,\" he noted.

\"I am,\" she declared.

\"Well, you shouldn\'t be,\" he warned.

\"Why shouldn\'t I be?\".

He looked directly into her eyes, and for a fleeting moment, she saw
something she had never truly seen before. It wasn\'t the cheerful,
sarcastic software engineer she had befriended. It wasn\'t her loving
boyfriend, nor was it even the ancient, powerful Rakshasa. It was just a
deeply frightened, vulnerable man.

\"What if I\'m wrong to trust that safety?\" he asked quietly. The heavy
question lingered unresolved in the air between them.

Chetana felt her heart tighten with profound empathy. This wasn\'t mere
stubbornness, nor was it misplaced arrogance. This was a deep-seated
fear that had existed for so incredibly long that it had structurally
become a core part of who he was.

She physically moved closer to him. \"You\'re not alone in this,\" she
promised.

Advik looked away, unable to meet her gaze.

She continued, her voice steady. \"Whatever this strange fear is, we
will figure it out together.\".

For a long moment, he didn\'t respond at all. Then, he forced a small,
tight smile that didn\'t even come close to reaching his eyes. \"Can we
please talk about something else now?\" he asked.

\"Coward,\" she teased gently.

\"Absolutely,\" he agreed without hesitation.

Despite the light joke, Chetana astutely noticed something crucial. The
exact moment the conversation actively moved away from the topic of
Vishnu, Advik visibly relaxed his posture, acting as though he had just
narrowly escaped a physical danger. And that realization frightened her
far more than the actual fear itself. Because for the very first time
since meeting him, she had discovered something significantly stronger
than his unwavering confidence. She had found a true weakness---a heavy,
invisible burden that had been ruthlessly haunting him long before she
had ever entered his life.

And she had absolutely no intention of passively letting him carry that
heavy burden all alone.

During the following weekend, Chetana travelled to visit her parents at
their home. She found her mother busy cooking in the kitchen, while her
younger brother, Chaitanya, was completely absorbed in playing games on
his mobile phone. Meanwhile, her father predictably sat in the quiet
living room with a heavy book resting comfortably on his lap. It was
just as usual; some things truly never changed.

Chetana walked over and sat down right beside him.

Jagannath looked up from the dense page, immediately reading her
expression. \"You look incredibly thoughtful today,\" he noted.

\"I am,\" she admitted.

\"Is there office trouble?\" he asked, concerned.

\"No,\" she shook her head.

\"Then what is it?\" he prompted.

Chetana hesitated, struggling to formulate her thoughts. She obviously
couldn\'t exactly just blurt out: \"My loving boyfriend is actually a
secret Rakshasa who can literally transform into Lord Narasimha.\". That
particular, world-shattering conversation would definitely require a
little more preparation on her part.

Instead, she cautiously asked, \"Can I ask you something theoretical?\".

\"Of course,\" he agreed.

\"Can Rakshasas naturally be afraid of Bhagwan Vishnu?\" she asked.

Jagannath raised an inquisitive eyebrow, as the highly specific question
had clearly caught the scholar off guard. \"That is a rather remarkably
specific question to ask out of nowhere,\" he observed.

\"I was just feeling curious,\" she lied smoothly.

\"Hmm,\" he murmured, thoughtfully closing his book to give her his full
attention. \"Are you asking traditionally speaking?\".

\"Yes,\" she confirmed.

\"Well, yes, many prominent Rakshasas deeply feared Vishnu,\" he
explained.

\"Why exactly?\" she asked.

\"Primarily because Vishnu repeatedly intervened with divine force
whenever powerful Rakshasas aggressively threatened the delicate balance
of the world,\" he elaborated.

Chetana listened quietly as Jagannath continued his theological
explanation.

\"In the ancient Puranas, Vishnu is very often portrayed as the supreme
one who destroys unchecked arrogance,\" he lectured gently. \"He is the
definitive one who forcefully restores balance, and the one who
decisively intervenes when raw power unfortunately becomes widespread
oppression.\".

Chetana nodded in understanding, as that much basic information she
already knew from her upbringing.

\"But you must understand that fear isn\'t simply automatic,\" Jagannath
added thoughtfully. \"Not every single Rakshasa blindly feared Him.\".

\"Then why would one particular individual be so completely terrified?\"
she pressed.

Jagannath thought deeply for a moment, stroking his chin. \"Sometimes,
profound fear simply isn\'t logical,\" he theorized. \"Sometimes, people
unknowingly inherit ancient stories and traumas. Sometimes they just
grow up entirely believing something to be an absolute truth without
question. And sometimes, a primal instinct powerfully convinces them
that a mortal danger exists even when it objectively doesn\'t.\". He
smiled slightly at the philosophical complexity of it all. \"The mind is
a very strange thing, Chetana.\".

Chetana remained incredibly thoughtful after hearing his profound
words---very, very thoughtful.

Later that same evening, she picked up her phone and boldly called
Advik. The line rang twice, then three times, then four agonising times
before he finally answered.

\"Hello,\" he mumbled, his deep voice sounding incredibly raspy and
sleepy.

\"Are you currently sleeping?\" she asked.

\"Obviously,\" he grumbled. \"Chetana, do you have any idea what time it
is?\".

\"It\'s exactly seven in the evening,\" she informed him cheerfully.

\"Exactly,\" he said, as if that proved his point.

\"Normal people are usually awake right now,\" she teased.

\"I\'ve already extensively explained this unfair societal
discrimination against dedicated night-shift employees,\" he complained.

Chetana laughed brightly, genuinely enjoying the fact that their
familiar, effortless humour had momentarily returned. Then, she
transitioned to her main purpose. \"Can I quickly ask you something
important?\".

There was a heavy, suspicious pause on the line. \"Chetana.\".

\"Hmm?\".

\"Literally every single time you say that exact phrase, my life
immediately becomes significantly more complicated,\" he pointed out.

\"Just answer anyway,\" she demanded playfully.

A long-suffering, highly dramatic sigh emerged loudly from the other
side of the connection. \"Fine. What is it?\".

\"Have you ever actually gone inside a Vishnu temple?\" she asked.

Silence. Absolute, instant, chilling silence.

The drastic change in his mood was so sudden and intense that it
genuinely startled her.

\"Advik?\" she prompted.

\"Why are we unnecessarily discussing this topic again?\" he asked
coldly.

\"Just answer the question,\" she insisted.

\"No,\" he said flatly.

\"You have literally never entered one?\" she clarified.

\"No.\".

\"Not even once in your entire life?\".

\"No,\" he repeated stubbornly.

\"But why?\".

\"Because I simply don\'t want to,\" he deflected.

\"That is an excuse, not an answer,\" she scolded.

\"Well, it is the only answer you are ever getting from me,\" he snapped
back.

Chetana frowned deeply. \"Advik.\".

\"No.\".

\"You haven\'t even heard my next question yet,\" she pointed out.

\"The answer is still no,\" he insisted.

\"You are absolutely impossible,\" she groaned.

\"Correct,\" he agreed.

She sighed heavily in frustration before intelligently deciding to
change her tactics. \"Tell me this, then: What exactly happens to you
physically when you go near one?\".

The vulnerable response came immediately. \"My heart starts rapidly
racing,\" he admitted.

Chetana blinked, as that vivid physiological description wasn\'t the
vague, mystical answer she had expected at all.

Advik continued, his voice dropping quietly. \"I become incredibly
restless. I instantly feel this overwhelming urge to just turn around
and run. I just feel like immediately leaving the area,\" he explained.
His deep voice lowered even further, filled with shame. \"Sometimes, I
can just faintly hear the temple bells ringing from far away, and I
still inexplicably feel completely uncomfortable,\" he confessed.

Any lingering trace of their earlier humour was entirely gone
now---completely and utterly gone.

Chetana listened carefully, her heart breaking for him. She realized
that this physical reaction wasn\'t based on hatred, nor was it born of
arrogance. It simply sounded disturbingly similar to a severe panic
attack.

\"Advik\...\" she whispered soothingly.

\"I\'m fine,\" he insisted defensively.

\"No, you are really not,\" she argued gently.

\"I\'m managing it,\" he claimed.

\"Merely managing it is not the same thing as being fine,\" she pointed
out.

The heavy silence that followed her statement lasted for several
agonizing seconds. When Advik finally spoke again, for the very first
time, there was raw, genuine frustration bleeding into his voice.

\"Why does this issue even matter so much to you?\" he demanded.

\"Because you are terrified and scared,\" she answered honestly.

\"So what?\" he challenged.

\"So, I genuinely do not like seeing you scared,\" she confessed.

The phone line instantly went quiet again---very, very quiet. When he
finally found the courage to speak again, his rough voice was noticeably
softer and much more vulnerable.

\"Chetana\...\" he pleaded.

\"Hmm?\".

\"Please, just leave this alone,\" he begged.

She frowned deeply at his request. \"No.\".

A tired, affectionate laugh finally escaped him. \"I knew you would say
that,\" he admitted.

\"You would do the exact same thing for me,\" she justified.

\"Probably,\" he conceded.

\"Definitely,\" she corrected.

\"Probably,\" he stubbornly repeated.

\"Definitely,\" she finalized.

For the very first time during that intense conversation, he actually
laughed properly, allowing the suffocating tension to finally ease
slightly. However, it only eased slightly. Because then, Chetana boldly
said something that made his laughter abruptly and permanently stop.

\"One day, Advik, I am personally taking you to a Vishnu temple,\" she
declared.

The shocked silence that immediately followed was profound and absolute.
Several tense seconds painfully passed.

Then he spoke. \"No.\".

\"Coward,\" she challenged.

\"No,\" he repeated.

\"You haven\'t even properly thought about it yet,\" she argued.

\"No,\" he said again.

\"Advik,\" she warned.

\"No.\". She could practically imagine him frantically shaking his head
in denial on the other end of the line. \"Absolutely not,\" he firmly
stated.

\"Why?\" she sighed.

\"Because I genuinely enjoy being alive,\" he proclaimed.

Chetana rolled her eyes at the ceiling. \"You are just being dramatic
now,\" she accused.

\"I am being cautious,\" he defended.

\"You\'re being completely ridiculous,\" she countered.

\"I\'m being alive,\" he maintained stubbornly.

She laughed aloud despite herself, but beneath the familiar, sarcastic
humour, she sharply noticed something deeply unsettling. He wasn\'t
actually joking. Not really. A massive part of him genuinely, truly
believed that simply entering a Vishnu temple would violently end his
existence.

And that startling realization only served to strengthen her stubborn
resolve. Because whatever terrible burden he had been silently carrying
since his childhood---whatever ancient, irrational fear had rooted
itself so deeply inside his mind---she was definitively going to help
him overcome it. She would do it even if he stubbornly fought her every
single step of the way.

Far away from her, safely inside his Pune apartment, Advik abruptly
ended the call. Then, he sat completely quietly on the edge of his bed.
The room was pitch dark and entirely silent. For a long, agonizing
moment, he just stared blankly at absolutely nothing. A Vishnu temple.
The very terrifying thought of it made his broad chest physically
tighten.

He deeply hated that instinctive fear. He hated how completely
irrational it sounded when spoken aloud, and he absolutely hated how
impossible it was to logically explain to anyone. Yet, despite his
immense power, it stubbornly remained, proving to be as constant and
inescapable as his own shadow.

Finally, feeling exhausted, he lay back heavily on the bed.

\"Not happening,\" he muttered decisively to himself.

Outside his window, the bright sun slowly disappeared entirely below the
horizon, signaling the start of his preferred hours. And somewhere far,
far away in the city, a single temple bell rang clearly through the
evening air.

Advik immediately grabbed a thick pillow and pulled it tightly over his
head, desperately trying to block out the sound, acting as if that
simple gesture would somehow actually help protect him from the divine.
