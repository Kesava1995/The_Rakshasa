**Chapter 10: The Burden That Was Never There**

Several peaceful months had passed since the violent events in Ranchi.
Life had finally become much quieter for everyone involved. Arjun\'s
sugar factory was functioning smoothly once again, and he had thankfully
stopped calling every single week with new, escalating problems. The
corrupt MLA had wisely decided that some battles were simply not worth
fighting. And for the very first time in a remarkably long while, Advik
had begun to feel that his life was completely normal again. At least,
it was as normal as life could possibly be for a Rakshasa.

Marriage suited him perfectly. More accurately, marriage with Chetana
suited him. She filled his previously quiet apartment with a vibrant
warmth he had never even realized was missing. She passionately argued
with him, mercilessly teased him, and routinely stole food directly from
his plate. Somehow, she managed to make him feel completely, wonderfully
human, despite knowing exactly what kind of ancient creature he truly
was.

Which was exactly why he had become highly suspicious the very moment
she flatly refused to tell him where they were going that morning.

\"Chetana,\" Advik prompted.

\"No,\" she replied instantly.

\"I haven\'t asked anything yet,\" he pointed out.

\"You were about to,\" she countered smoothly.

Advik sighed in defeat. \"You know me entirely too well\".

\"I married you,\" she reasoned. \"It would be highly concerning if I
didn\'t\".

In afternoon, they were currently walking together through the bustling,
vibrant streets of Srirangam. The bright afternoon sun beautifully
illuminated the ancient structures towering all around them. Countless
devout pilgrims moved steadily through the narrow streets, street
vendors loudly sold fragrant flowers, and heavy temple bells
occasionally rang out in the distance. Advik frowned deeply. Something
felt wrong---very, very wrong. He slowly turned his gaze toward the
horizon and instantly froze in his tracks.

Towering majestically above the entire town stood an enormous,
intimidating gopuram. His stomach immediately tightened into a painful
knot.

\"No,\" he whispered.

Chetana deliberately ignored him and continued walking. \"No,\" he
repeated.

She continued walking. \"Chetana\". Still walking. \"Chetana!\" he
called out louder.

She finally stopped and looked back at him. \"Yes?\".

His trembling finger pointed accusingly toward the gigantic temple
tower.

\"What is that?\".

She followed his finger with her eyes. \"Oh\".

\"\'Oh\'?\" he echoed.

\"That\'s the Sri Ranganathaswamy Temple,\" she informed him cheerfully.

Advik stared blankly at her. Then he stared at the massive temple. Then
back at her. Then at the temple once again.

\"No,\" he declared firmly.

\"What no?\" she asked.

\"No,\" he repeated stubbornly.

\"Chetana, that\'s not an answer\".

\"It is today\". She smiled brightly, but Advik definitely did not share
her amusement. He was rapidly beginning to understand exactly why she
had stubbornly refused to explain the purpose of this trip.

\"You tricked me,\" he accused.

\"I guided you,\" she corrected smoothly.

\"You tricked me,\" he insisted.

\"I prefer the term \'guided\',\".

\"You brought me directly to a Vishnu temple,\" he stated.

\"Correct,\" she nodded.

\"You brought me to one of the biggest Vishnu temples in existence,\" he
elaborated, his panic rising. \"Also correct,\" she confirmed.

\"Chetana,\" he said, deadpan. \"Yes?\" \"I want a divorce\".

She burst out laughing, and the fact that she found his terror amusing
made the dire situation feel even worse.

\"I\'m serious,\" he warned.

\"No, you\'re not,\" she dismissed.

\"I absolutely am,\" he argued.

\"You\'ll get over it\".

\"I won\'t\".

\"You will\".

\"I won\'t\".

\"You will\".

Advik stared intensely at her, and Chetana stared right back; neither of
them moved an inch. After several tense seconds, Advik pointed firmly
toward the road behind them.

\"I think we should leave immediately,\" he suggested.

\"No\".

\"I think that\'s a wonderful idea,\" he pressed.

\"No\".

\"I think it may be the greatest idea humanity has ever produced,\" he
pleaded.

\"No\".

\"Chetana,\" he begged.

\"No\".

Advik looked toward the temple again. The massive, sacred structure
seemed far larger and more imposing now. His ancient instincts screamed
violently at him, demanding that every single part of him needed to turn
around and leave immediately. The feeling was entirely irrational, and
he logically knew it was irrational, but simply knowing that fact did
not help him; it never had. For as long as he could possibly remember,
Bhagwan Vishnu had utterly terrified him. It wasn\'t because he hated
Vishnu, nor was it because he philosophically disagreed with Him or
wanted to actively oppose Him. He simply feared Him deeply,
instinctively, and completely---exactly like vulnerable prey sensing a
lethal predator, or a small rabbit hearing a hungry wolf nearby.

His broad chest tightened painfully. \"Chetana,\" his voice dropped to a
much quieter, vulnerable register. \"Please\".

The playful humour instantly disappeared from her expression. She
reached out and took his large hand in hers. \"I know,\" she said
softly.

Those two simple words surprised him, because she truly did know. Most
ordinary people would have laughed at his irrational dread, dismissed it
entirely, or called it completely ridiculous. She never had. She had
always treated his profound fear as something very real, even when she
didn\'t fully understand it, and even when he couldn\'t logically
explain it to her.

She squeezed his hand reassuringly. \"I know you\'re scared,\" she
acknowledged.

Advik looked away, ashamed. \"I don\'t want to go inside\".

\"I know\".

\"What if---\" he started, then abruptly stopped himself.

\"What if what?\" she asked softly. He hesitated for a long moment
before finally speaking his deepest insecurity.

\"What if I\'ve been right all these years?\".

For a moment, neither of them spoke. The busy crowd flowed around them,
and the temple bells rang rhythmically in the distance. Finally, Chetana
stepped closer to him.

\"What if you\'re wrong?\" she challenged gently.

Advik didn\'t answer, primarily because that distinct possibility
frightened him just as much. If he was truly wrong, then he had
tragically spent his entire life fiercely fearing something that had
never actually wanted to hurt him. Somehow, accepting that massive
misunderstanding felt just as uncomfortable.

Chetana smiled gently. \"Come with me\".

\"No\".

\"Advik\".

\"No\".

\"Advik\".

\"No\".

\"Advik\".

He growled. He actually growled---a low, intimidating, tiger-like rumble
that escaped uncontrollably from deep within his throat. Several nearby
pilgrims looked over in alarm.

Chetana simply blinked, completely unfazed, before folding her arms
across her chest. \"Are you entirely done?\" she asked.

He offered another low growl in response.

She raised a single unimpressed eyebrow. \"Do you honestly think I\'m
five years old?\".

The intimidating growling stopped immediately. \"Maybe,\" he muttered.

She stepped forward and smacked his arm. \"Ouch,\" he complained.
\"Good,\" she stated.

\"I was merely expressing myself,\" he defended.

\"You were being dramatic,\" she corrected.

\"I am a Rakshasa,\" he pointed out proudly.

\"You are a drama king,\" she countered effortlessly.

Advik looked highly offended, but Chetana simply grabbed his hand
tightly. \"Come on\". He resisted. She pulled. He resisted harder. She
pulled even harder. Several passing pilgrims watched with deep amusement
as a fully grown, powerful man was quite literally dragged toward one of
the holiest temples in India by his determined wife.

\"Chetana\".

\"No\".

\"Chetana\".

\"No\".

\"Chetana\".

\"No\".

\"People are staring,\" he tried.

\"Good,\" she replied.

Finally, Advik realized he wasn\'t going to win. He never won arguments
with her, a fact that still remained deeply, terribly unfair. Wearing
the grim expression of a condemned man walking toward his own execution,
Advik surrendered and followed her. Together, they finally crossed the
threshold and entered.

The chaotic noise of the outside world gradually faded away into a
sacred calm. Cool stone corridors stretched endlessly before them, while
ancient, intricately carved pillars stood silently beneath the towering
temple ceiling. The atmosphere inside felt profoundly
different---calmer, much older, and as though centuries of pure,
unbroken devotion had settled deeply into the very stones themselves.

Advik\'s heartbeat accelerated wildly. One step. Another. Another. The
primal fear remained, heavy and suffocating. Yet, surprisingly,
something else had begun to appear alongside it: a quiet, undeniable
curiosity.

Eventually, they reached the inner sanctum. Devotees stood around them
quietly, priests performed their sacred rituals, and the air carried the
heavy, sweet scent of burning incense and fresh flowers. And there,
resting magnificently before him, was Lord Ranganatha---the supreme
deity reclining atop the cosmic serpent, Adi Shesha.

Advik simply stared. The immense fear surged up within him one final,
terrifying time. Then---

Silence. It was not an external silence, but a profound internal
silence. The fear abruptly vanished. It was completely, miraculously
gone, breaking instantly like a fragile chain suddenly snapping. His
golden eyes widened in sheer disbelief. For the very first time in his
entire life\... there was absolutely nothing. There was no dread, no
suffocating panic, and no primal instinct violently screaming at him to
run away. There was only immense, unshakeable peace.

Then, a divine voice spoke. It did not speak through his ears, but
resonated clearly and directly through his mind. It was calm,
unimaginably ancient, and incredibly gentle.

*\"Why are you afraid of me?\"*.

Advik froze completely. He knew immediately, without a single doubt,
exactly who was speaking to him. The divine voice continued.

*\"I have watched you since your birth.\"*. His throat tightened with
emotion. *\"You need not fear me.\"*.

The voice carried absolutely no anger. No condemnation. No hostility.
There was only absolute certainty.

*\"You will not be harmed.\"*.

The heavy, crushing burden that had relentlessly followed him since
childhood rapidly began dissolving into nothingness.

*\"Live your life,\"* the voice instructed gently, seeming almost fond
and amused. *\"Protect those you care for.\"*.

The remaining fragments of his lifelong terror continued crumbling away.

*\"And do not become a burden to the world.\"*.

With that final command, the last lingering shadows of fear disappeared
completely, and the divine voice peacefully faded away, leaving only
perfect serenity behind in his mind.

Advik stood entirely motionless, his eyes growing wet with unshed tears.
For several long moments, he simply stared in awe at the reclining
deity. Then, very slowly, he smiled. It was a genuine, beautiful
smile---perhaps the very first completely unburdened smile he had ever
worn in his entire life.

Beside him, Chetana watched him quietly. She already knew; she could
physically see that the suffocating fear was finally gone. Without
speaking a single word, she gently reached out for his hand. He squeezed
it back with immense gratitude.

Together, they walked back outside into the warm evening. The vast sky
had already begun turning a brilliant shade of orange. For several
peaceful minutes, neither of them spoke, simply enjoying the tranquil
atmosphere.

\"How do you feel?\" Chetana finally asked, breaking the silence.

Advik laughed softly, a sound so free that it surprised even him.
\"Stupid,\" he admitted.

She smiled warmly. \"Why?\".

\"I spent my entire life being absolutely terrified for nothing,\" he
confessed.

\"Not for nothing,\" she corrected gently. He looked at her, and she
squeezed his hand affectionately. \"It brought you here\". Advik laughed
again, this time much more freely. The fear was gone---actually, truly
gone. For the very first time in his existence, he felt completely and
utterly at peace with himself and the world.

Then, Chetana abruptly stopped walking. \"Hmm?\" he asked, turning to
her. She looked unusually nervous, an expression that immediately spiked
his protective worry. \"Chetana?\". \"There is something I really need
to tell you,"She began hesitantly. Advik blinked, highly concerned.
\"What happened?\". \"Nothing bad,\" she quickly assured him. His worry
only increased. \"Then why do you look so nervous?\".

She took a deep, steadying breath. Then she smiled---a small, radiant,
absolutely beautiful smile---and spoke three life-altering words.

\"I\'m pregnant\".

For several long seconds, Advik simply stared at her. His highly
advanced brain completely stopped functioning. Pregnant. The word echoed
continuously through his stunned mind. Pregnant. Pregnant. Pregnant.

Then, the miraculous realization finally arrived. His golden eyes
widened drastically.

\"Chetana!\" he gasped. She nodded, her eyes shining. \"Yes\".
\"Chetana!\" he yelled. She laughed joyfully. Before she could even say
another word, he reached out and effortlessly lifted her right off the
ground.

\"Chetana!\". \"I think you already said my name,\" she teased through
her laughter. \"We\'re having a baby!\" he cheered. \"That is generally
how pregnancy works,\" she joked. \"We\'re having a baby!\" he repeated
in pure awe. \"Yes\". \"We\'re having a baby!\". \"Yes\". \"We\'re---\".
\"Advik,\" she interrupted gently. \"Yes?\". \"Put me down\".

He carefully and tenderly set her back down onto the street, then
immediately wrapped his strong arms around her in a fierce, loving hug.
She hugged him back just as tightly.

The cool evening breeze moved peacefully through the ancient streets of
Srirangam. Pilgrims continued their spiritual walks around them, and the
temple bells rang beautifully in the distance. For the very first time
in his entire, complicated life, Advik felt absolutely no fear. There
was only pure, overwhelming gratitude: a brilliant wife whom he deeply
loved, a beautiful future child on the way, a completely peaceful heart,
and an imaginary burden that had never truly existed in the first place.

Together, hand in hand, they happily began walking toward whatever
wonderful adventure came next.
